What is Schizophrenia?

Definition: Schizophrenia is a chronic brain disorder that affects how a person thinks, feels, and behaves. People with schizophrenia may experience hallucinations, delusions, and extremely disordered thinking.

Key Characteristics:

  • Distortions in thinking
  • Distortions in perception
  • Distortions in emotions
  • Distortions in language
  • Distortions in sense of self
  • Distortions in behavior
1%
of the population affected worldwide

Historical Perspective

1887 - Emil Kraepelin

First described "dementia praecox" - a progressive deteriorating psychotic disorder

1911 - Eugen Bleuler

Coined the term "schizophrenia" meaning "split mind"

1950s - First Antipsychotics

Chlorpromazine revolutionized treatment

1980 - DSM-III

Standardized diagnostic criteria established

2013 - DSM-5

Introduction of Schizophrenia Spectrum concept

Schizophrenia Spectrum Disorders

Disorder Duration Key Features
Schizotypal Personality Disorder Lifelong pattern Odd beliefs, behavior, and speech
Delusional Disorder ≥1 month Fixed false beliefs without other symptoms
Brief Psychotic Disorder 1 day - 1 month Sudden onset, often stress-related
Schizophreniform Disorder 1-6 months Same as schizophrenia but shorter
Schizophrenia ≥6 months Full syndrome with functional decline
Schizoaffective Disorder Variable Schizophrenia + mood episodes

Positive Symptoms

Experiences that are "added" to normal functioning

Hallucinations

  • Auditory (most common - 70%)
  • Visual (15-20%)
  • Tactile (feeling sensations)
  • Olfactory (smelling odors)
  • Gustatory (tasting)

Disorganized Speech

  • Loose associations
  • Tangential thinking
  • Word salad
  • Neologisms

Delusions

  • Persecutory (most common)
  • Referential
  • Grandiose
  • Religious
  • Somatic

Disorganized Behavior

  • Unpredictable agitation
  • Bizarre postures
  • Excessive motor activity
  • Echopraxia

Attention & memory issues

DSM-5 Diagnostic Criteria

Criterion A: Two or more of the following for ≥1 month:

  1. Delusions
  2. Hallucinations
  3. Disorganized speech
  4. Grossly disorganized or catatonic behavior
  5. Negative symptoms

Additional Criteria:

  • B: Significant functional decline
  • C: Continuous signs for ≥6 months
  • D: Exclusion of other disorders
  • E: Not due to substances
  • F: If autism history, prominent delusions/hallucinations required

Neurobiological Factors

Genetic Factors

Risk increases with genetic proximity to affected individual

Brain Abnormalities

  • Enlarged ventricles
  • Reduced gray matter
  • Frontal lobe dysfunction
  • Temporal lobe abnormalities
  • Hippocampal volume reduction

Neurotransmitter Imbalances:

• Dopamine (excess)
• Glutamate (deficiency)
• GABA (reduced)
• Serotonin (altered)

10 / 20

Environmental Risk Factors

Prenatal & Perinatal

  • Maternal infections (influenza, toxoplasmosis)
  • Malnutrition during pregnancy
  • Birth complications
  • Low birth weight
  • Hypoxia during delivery

Environmental Stressors

  • Urban upbringing (2-3x risk)
  • Migration stress
  • Social isolation
  • Childhood trauma
  • Cannabis use in adolescence

Social Factors

  • Family dysfunction
  • Expressed emotion (criticism)
  • Social defeat
  • Discrimination
  • Poverty

Timing Factors

  • Season of birth (winter/spring)
  • Advanced paternal age
  • Adolescent stress
  • Critical developmental periods

Treatment Approaches

Pharmacological Treatment

First-Generation Antipsychotics:

  • Haloperidol
  • Chlorpromazine
  • Fluphenazine

Second-Generation Antipsychotics:

  • Risperidone
  • Olanzapine
  • Quetiapine
  • Aripiprazole
  • Clozapine (treatment-resistant)

Psychosocial Interventions

  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)
  • Family therapy
  • Social skills training
  • Cognitive remediation
  • Vocational rehabilitation
  • Assertive Community Treatment (ACT)
70%
Response rate with combined treatment

Recent Research: Genetics

Study: Nature (2022) - Schizophrenia Working Group

Title: "Common schizophrenia alleles are enriched in mutation-intolerant genes"

Sample Size: 76,755 cases and 243,649 controls

Key Findings:

  • 287 genomic loci associated with schizophrenia
  • 120 genes directly implicated
  • Synaptic organization pathways identified
  • Neuronal excitability genes involved
  • 10 genes with therapeutic potential

Case Study: Early-Onset Schizophrenia

Patient Profile: "James"

Age: 22 years old | Gender: Male | Onset: Age 18

Age 16-17: Prodromal Phase

Social withdrawal, declining grades, unusual beliefs about classmates

Age 18: First Episode

Auditory hallucinations, paranoid delusions, hospitalization

Age 19-20: Treatment

Started on risperidone, CBT, family therapy

Age 21-22: Recovery

Returned to college part-time, minimal symptoms, good medication adherence

Case Study: Treatment-Resistant Schizophrenia

Patient Profile: "Sarah"

Age: 35 years old | Gender: Female | Onset: Age 23 | Duration: 12 years

Medication Trial Duration Response
Risperidone 8 months Partial response, weight gain
Olanzapine 6 months Minimal improvement
Aripiprazole 10 months No significant change
Clozapine Ongoing (2 years) 70% symptom reduction

Key Learning: Clozapine remains the gold standard for treatment-resistant schizophrenia, with regular blood monitoring required.

Prognosis and Long-term Outcomes

Favorable Prognostic Factors:

  • Later age of onset
  • Acute onset
  • Good premorbid functioning
  • Married/strong social support
  • Positive symptoms predominant
  • Good treatment adherence

Poor Prognostic Factors:

  • Early age of onset
  • Insidious onset
  • Poor premorbid functioning
  • Social isolation
  • Negative symptoms predominant
  • Substance abuse

Key Takeaways

1. Complex Disorder

Schizophrenia involves multiple brain systems and has diverse presentations

2. Spectrum Concept

Recognition of various related disorders with different durations and severities

3. Multifactorial Etiology

Genetic vulnerability + environmental factors = increased risk

4. Treatment Works

Combination of medication and psychosocial interventions most effective

5. Early Intervention

Better outcomes with prompt treatment during first episode

6. Hope for Recovery

Many individuals lead fulfilling lives with proper treatment and support
